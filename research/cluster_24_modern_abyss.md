## Part 1: Analysis & Synthesis (Steps 4-13)

### A. Carl Jung: The Red Book
*   **The Engine:** The Night Sea Journey. The underworld is not a location; it is the "Collective Unconscious"—a repository of archetypal images that must be confronted to achieve wholeness (*The Red Book*, *Man and His Symbols*).
*   **Aesthetic Motifs:**
    *   *The Mandalas:* Geometric patterns representing the "Center."
    *   *The Serpent:* The primordial wisdom/threat.
    *   *The Shadow:* The rejected, hidden self.
*   **Epistemology:** *Observation is Integration.* To see the Shadow is to begin to own it.

### B. Sigmund Freud: The Uncanny Valley
*   **The Engine:** The Return of the Repressed. Horror arises when the familiar (*Heimlich*) reveals its hidden, strange (*Unheimlich*) nature (*Das Unheimliche*).
*   **Aesthetic Motifs:**
    *   *The Double:* The doppelganger as a threat to identity.
    *   *The Automaton:* The uncertainty of whether an object is alive or dead.
    *   *The Loop:* Repeating an act or finding oneself back at the same location.
*   **Epistemology:** *The Familiar is a Mask.* Beneath the "Home" lies the "Strange."

### C. H.P. Lovecraft: The Cosmic Indifference
*   **The Engine:** The Great Old Ones. Horror as the realization that the universe is vast, ancient, and entirely indifferent to human existence. Knowledge does not lead to truth; it leads to madness (*The Call of Cthulhu*, *At the Mountains of Madness*).
*   **Aesthetic Motifs:**
    *   *Non-Euclidean Geometry:* Architecture that defies human spatial logic.
    *   *The Tentacle:* The biological "Wrongness" of the cosmic other.
    *   *The Indescribable:* The failure of language to capture the horror.
*   **Epistemology:** *Knowing is Destruction.* To understand the true nature of reality is to lose the capacity to inhabit it.
